	•	Yes, risk management + circuit breakers can dramatically reduce blow-ups.
	•	No, they do not make copy trading “safe” because your main risks are execution mismatch, tail events, and settlement/platform risk.
	•	If you want “as close to safe as possible”, you need hard caps, liquidity filters, limit-only execution, and rules that force you to skip most trades.

⸻

What circuit breakers actually protect you from

They help with:
	•	Runaway automation bugs (infinite loop, wrong side orders, wrong market).
	•	Overexposure (too many correlated positions).
	•	Sudden adverse moves (if you include mark-to-market stops).
	•	Death by a thousand cuts (daily loss limits).

They do not solve:
	•	You getting worse fills than the account you copy (the core copy-trading problem).
	•	Resolution/oracle disputes (you can be “right” and still lose).
	•	Liquidity traps (you can’t exit at your stop price if order book is thin).
	•	Adversarial behavior (you become exit liquidity if you chase their trades).

⸻

“Near-minimum-risk” blueprint for copying

1) Exposure caps (these are non-negotiable)
	•	Per-market max loss: e.g. 0.25% to 1% of bankroll.
	•	Daily max loss: e.g. 0.5% to 2%.
	•	Weekly max loss: e.g. 2% to 5%.
	•	Max open risk (sum of worst-case losses): e.g. 2% to 4%.

If any cap hits, halt trading.

2) Liquidity and slippage gates (skip most markets)

Only copy if all are true:
	•	Spread below a threshold (example: <= 1–2 cents in winner-take-all style pricing).
	•	Sufficient depth at/near best bid/ask (example: depth supports your size with minimal slippage).
	•	Recent volume above threshold.
If the market fails the gate, do not trade, no matter how “good” the trader is.

3) Limit-only execution

Market orders are how copiers get farmed.
	•	Place limit orders at your target price.
	•	If not filled within a short window, cancel.
	•	Do not “chase” unless your system explicitly allows a small controlled chase (and counts it as slippage cost).

4) Copy delay + price improvement rule

A simple but effective rule:
	•	After detecting their trade, wait X seconds (or a random range).
	•	Only enter if price is no worse than your max deviation from their entry (or from your own fair price estimate).
If you cannot get the price, skip.

This single rule eliminates a lot of “exit liquidity” scenarios.

5) Volatility breaker

If the market moves too fast, you are late by definition.
	•	If price changes by more than (example) Y cents in Z seconds, halt trading that market for N minutes.

6) Correlation breaker

Copiers blow up by taking the same bet repeatedly across similar markets.
	•	Cap exposure to one theme (e.g., “crypto up/down”, “election”) regardless of market count.

7) Resolution-risk breaker

Skip markets with:
	•	Ambiguous rules.
	•	“Subjective” outcomes.
	•	History of disputes.
Copy trading is already execution-disadvantaged; don’t add resolution ambiguity.

⸻

A realistic “can I get it to almost zero?” answer

You can get probability of ruin close to zero by trading tiny size with strict caps, but you cannot get risk to zero because:
	•	Some losses can happen even with perfect risk management (resolution/platform issues).
	•	Your expected edge might be negative due to execution (you’ll still lose slowly).

So the correct framing is:
	•	“Can I cap downside?” Yes.
	•	“Can I make copying reliably profitable?” Not guaranteed, often no, unless you have an additional edge (better execution, better pricing, better selection).

⸻

The one thing you must measure before trusting this

If you don’t measure this, you are guessing:

“Follower slippage” = (your average entry price - their average entry price) + (your average exit price - their exit price) + fees.

If follower slippage is consistently negative by more than the trader’s edge, you lose even with perfect risk controls.

Risks and mitigations (short, complete)

1) Execution mismatch (you copy late, worse fills)

Risk: You enter/exit worse than the wallet you copy, turning their edge into your loss.
Mitigation:
	•	Limit-only entries and exits.
	•	Max slippage rule: if price moved beyond your threshold, skip.
	•	No chasing (no market orders, no “I must get in”).

2) Illiquidity and spread traps

Risk: You cannot enter/exit without paying big spread or moving the price. Stops do not work if there is no depth.
Mitigation:
	•	Copy only high-liquidity markets (tight spread, real depth, consistent volume).
	•	Size small relative to top-of-book depth.
	•	If book depth drops, halt that market.

3) Tail risk (rare events wipe many small gains)

Risk: Selling “insurance” style or copying traders who do can erase months of profits in one loss.
Mitigation:
	•	Per-market max loss (hard cap).
	•	Avoid profiles with high win rate + occasional massive drawdown.
	•	Prefer strategies with defined downside (don’t average down).

4) Resolution and rules ambiguity

Risk: Market resolves against you due to wording, sources, disputes, or unexpected interpretation.
Mitigation:
	•	Trade only markets with crisp resolution criteria and reputable, unambiguous sources.
	•	Avoid “subjective” markets and anything with unclear definitions.
	•	Smaller size in any market where you are not 100% confident about resolution mechanics.

5) Correlation risk (many bets are secretly the same bet)

Risk: You think you are diversified but everything moves together (e.g., multiple crypto markets).
Mitigation:
	•	Cap exposure by theme (crypto, election, sports).
	•	Max total open risk across all positions.

6) Overtrading and fees

Risk: Small edge gets eaten by fees and spread, especially if you trade frequently.
Mitigation:
	•	Prefer fewer, higher-quality trades.
	•	Enforce minimum expected edge after costs: “If edge < costs buffer, skip.”

7) Adversarial wallets (you become exit liquidity)

Risk: Some wallets can bait followers, or their activity is not meant to be copied.
Mitigation:
	•	Add random delay and require price-improvement (or skip).
	•	Copy only wallets that are not obviously “signal accounts” (sudden public attention, suspicious timing, pump patterns).

8) Automation and ops risk (bugs, outages, keys)

Risk: A bug or outage causes wrong orders, repeated orders, or inability to exit.
Mitigation:
	•	Circuit breakers (below).
	•	Kill switch on: repeated errors, abnormal order rate, abnormal slippage, API downtime.
	•	Separate keys, minimal permissions, secure storage, manual override.

9) Platform / counterparty / infrastructure risk

Risk: Downtime, settlement issues, or other platform-level failures can cause losses or stuck positions.
Mitigation:
	•	Keep position sizes small enough that a worst-case platform incident is survivable.
	•	Avoid holding large exposures through high-risk windows if you cannot manage them.

Minimal circuit breakers (use these if you want “2% style” conservative)
	•	Daily loss limit: stop trading for the day if hit.
	•	Weekly loss limit: stop for the week if hit.
	•	Per-market loss cap: never exceed it.
	•	Max open risk: sum of worst-case losses across positions.
	•	Volatility breaker: if price moves more than X in Y time, halt that market.
	•	Slippage breaker: if average slippage exceeds threshold, halt copying that wallet.
	•	Error-rate breaker: if N order/API errors in M minutes, halt all.

If you want “I’m happy with 2%”, you should expect to skip most copy signals and trade only when:
	•	market is liquid,
	•	your fill is close (limit fill),
	•	rules are clear,
	•	exposure fits your caps.
